## Pricing Psychology & Tier Strategies

### Pricing Psychology Principles

**1. Charm Pricing ($97 vs. $100)**
- Ending in 7 or 9 feels significantly cheaper
- $97 vs. $100 can increase conversions by 8-12%
- Use for entry-level products

**2. Anchoring**
- Show original price crossed out: ~~$297~~ $197
- Makes discount feel substantial
- Compare to alternatives: "A 1-on-1 coach costs $200/hour. This course is $197 total."

**3. Price Tiering (Good-Better-Best)**
- Offer 3 options
- Most people choose the middle option (~60%)
- Example:
  - **Basic:** $97 (Course only)
  - **Standard:** $197 (Course + bonuses + community) ‚Üê Most popular
  - **Premium:** $497 (Everything + 1-on-1 coaching)

**4. Value Stacking**
- List everything included with individual "values"
- Total value: $1,500+ ‚Üí Your price: $197
- Creates perception of huge discount

**5. Payment Plans**
- Lower barrier to entry
- Example: $497 ‚Üí 3 payments of $167
- Increase total price by 10-20% for payment plan (people still prefer it)

**6. Urgency & Scarcity**
- Early bird pricing (24-48 hours)
- Enrollment caps: "Only 50 spots available"
- Seasonal bonuses that expire
- Next cohort price increase: "Join now at $197. Next enrollment: $247"

**7. Decoy Pricing**
- Create an expensive option to make mid-tier look reasonable
- Example:
  - Course alone: $197
  - Course + coaching: $497
  - VIP day: $2,000 ‚Üê Makes $497 seem like a deal

### Ethical Upselling Best Practices

**Do:**
- Make upsells genuinely valuable and relevant
- Clearly explain what's included
- Offer fair discounts (not fake scarcity)
- Allow easy opt-out ("No thanks" button, no guilt)
- Apply upsell credits toward future purchases

**Don't:**
- Use dark patterns (hidden charges, confusing language)
- Offer irrelevant products just to make money
- Pressure or guilt-trip buyers
- Make false urgency claims
- Spam with constant upsells
